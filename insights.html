What a Film Set Teaches You About Corporate Video

Every corporate shoot we run borrows its discipline from feature film sets, not from marketing decks. A film set assumes the camera only rolls once things are right — blocking rehearsed, light metered, sound checked — because a wasted take costs the whole crew's time. Most corporate video looks like corporate video because it skips that discipline: a presenter reads off a teleprompter in front of whatever wall was available, and everyone hopes editing will fix it.

It won't, not fully. The single biggest quality jump we can make on any engineering or enterprise shoot has nothing to do with cameras — it's spending an extra hour on blocking and light before anyone says "action." That's a film-set habit, and it's the one we refuse to drop regardless of budget.

The Three-Act Structure Hiding in Your Company's Pitch Deck

Screenwriters have used the same three-act shape for a century: establish the world, introduce the problem that breaks it, resolve the problem in a way that changes something. Most technical pitch decks already have this structure by accident — a market context slide, a problem slide, a solution slide — but they present it as a list instead of a story, so the tension never builds and nobody remembers it an hour later.

When we turn a deck into a film, the content barely changes. What changes is sequencing and pace: we hold on the problem a beat longer than feels comfortable before we release it with the solution. That single edit decision is usually the difference between a video people watch politely and one they actually remember.

03 — Corporate Communication 3 min read Read the full note →

Why Most Corporate Videos Fail in the First Eight Seconds

Eight seconds is roughly how long a cold viewer gives an unfamiliar video before deciding whether to keep watching, and most corporate films spend that window on a logo animation and a stock drone shot of an office building. Neither tells the viewer why they should care. The films that hold attention open on a specific, concrete moment — a hand adjusting an instrument, a line of dialogue mid-conversation — because specificity reads as truth, and truth is what keeps a skeptical viewer in the frame.

Our rule for any corporate open: if the first shot could be swapped into a competitor's video without anyone noticing, it's the wrong first shot.

04 — Editing 4 min read Read the full note →

The Invisible Craft: What Great Editing Actually Does

Good editing is judged by what a viewer never notices — the cut they don't feel, the pacing that never drags, the moment a scene ends exactly one beat before it overstays its welcome. It's a craft built almost entirely out of subtraction: every cut is a decision to remove something, and the skill lives in knowing what to remove without the audience sensing the gap. Fourteen years into doing this for a living, the note that still improves a cut more than any other is the simplest one: what happens if we just lose this?

That instinct doesn't come from software. It comes from watching a scene fail in a hundred small ways until you can feel the failure coming before it happens.

05 — AI 4 min read Read the full note →

Where AI Belongs in the Edit Suite (And Where It Doesn't)

AI tools have quietly become useful for the unglamorous parts of post-production: transcript-based rough cuts, noise reduction, rotoscoping, dailies organization — tasks that are mechanical enough that automating them frees an editor to spend more time on the part of the job that actually requires judgment. We use them there, happily, because they buy back hours we'd rather spend on structure and rhythm.

Where we've drawn a hard line is generative replacement of actual footage, performance or voice — not out of nostalgia, but because the entire value of a documentary or a brand film is that it's evidence something real happened. The day an audience can't trust that a shot is real is the day the format stops working, for us and for everyone else in it.

06 — Visual Strategy 3 min read Read the full note →

Brand Films vs. Brand Ads: Knowing Which One You Need

The two get commissioned interchangeably and they shouldn't be — a brand ad is built to interrupt someone and make a claim in fifteen seconds; a brand film is built to be sought out and rewards three minutes of attention with an actual story. Most of the disappointment we see with corporate video happens when a client commissions one and briefs the other: a three-minute film cut down to fifteen seconds loses its story, and a fifteen-second ad stretched to three minutes loses its urgency.

Before any camera is booked, the honest first question is which of the two you're actually making — and where each one is going to run.

07 — Digital Marketing 3 min read Read the full note →

The Metric That Actually Predicts Whether Your Video Will Work

View counts flatter everyone and predict almost nothing. The number we actually watch on a client's behalf is average watch-through at the ten-second mark — because it's the earliest point where the audience has made a real decision about the content rather than just letting an autoplay run. A video that holds 70% of viewers past ten seconds will usually keep earning attention, and pairing that number with click-through on the very next scene tells us exactly where a cut is losing people, scene by scene.

Vanity metrics make a report look good for a week. Watch-through curves tell us what to fix before the next campaign.
